Evaluating a US Navy VR training program: Methodology and challenges

Ian Coffman --AnthroTronix, Inc.

Session Description

"The US Navy has recently implemented the Immersive Virtual Shipboard Environment (IVSE), an interactive, first-person perspective software replica of the Littoral Combat Ship (LCS) designed to simulate and largely supplant on-the-job training for certain engineering roles/ratings. The IVSE consists of a series of modules that each cover tasks related to routine maintenance/system alignment as well as adverse events (i.e., casualties) that may occur in deployed settings. Each module includes a series of training phases with diminishing degrees of guidance and culminates with a “watchstanding challenge” meant to simulate actual duty aboard the LCS. Under contract with the Office of Naval Research, we were tasked with designing a research program aimed at both confirming the underlying need for the IVSE as well as an evaluating its training effectiveness. In this presentation, we provide an overview of our methodology and highlight a few key challenges that we believe are potentially relevant to a variety of efforts aimed at evaluating novel VR training solutions. We will outline our approach to the determination of which outcome variables/metrics are best suited for the evaluation of the IVSE, which is an especially challenging issue since direct observation of training effectiveness (e.g., measurement of performance during actual warfare scenarios) is extremely difficult if not impossible. We will also discuss the importance of qualitative feedback from Sailors and their Non-Commissioned Officers to address any gaps in understanding resulting from difficulties in collecting “hard” data related to certifications/qualifications We will build on these observations to demonstrate the necessity of a hybrid quantitative/qualitative approach that is required in order to work within the constraints of operational military environments. There are two main issues we highlight as attendee takeaways: first, we stress that many (especially military) scenarios where VR training programs are utilized may not lend themselves to direct observation of training effectiveness, requiring evaluators to develop “surrogate” measures of effectiveness. Second, given that variables relevant to training effectiveness are not always directly quantifiable, qualitative feedback from actual users can be crucial to a more complete understanding of a VR training program’s effectiveness."

Bio

Ian Coffman is a research scientist at AnthroTronix, Inc., specializing in cognitive evaluation and applied statistics. He holds degrees in linguistics and cognitive science from the University of California, Berkeley and Johns Hopkins University, respectively.
